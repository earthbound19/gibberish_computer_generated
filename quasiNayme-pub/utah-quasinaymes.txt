==QUASINAYMES==

What is a "quasinayme?" A quasinayme is a nayme which is made up for sport e.g. to mock true naymes, or for morbid entertainment, etc.

Well, then, what's a "nayme?" A nayme is a real-life name, given to a real person, which should never have been given to that person, because it curses or burdens them unecessarily in one or more ways.

===NAYMES: AN EXTENDED LECTURE===

*DO NOT* name any child anything like the following, and if you already have, urge formal renaming to a respectable name!

=THE LISTS=

Most of the time from now on, I'll simply use the term "name," and you may tell from context when and whether that refers to a quasinayme, a nayme, or (an ordinary) name.

Relevant to the following key is the question: how did I make the quasinaymes?

I fed the lists of names from the "Utah Baby [mis-]Namer" web site through a marvelous, free computer program entitled "Zeta Centauri Trigram Generator," which brilliantly recombobulates words from any source text. I then filtered out generated words which were actually repeats of words in the source material (this took a bit of data wrangling which I won't explain at this writing), so that I had only unique ("CONTRIVED," remember) . . . quasinaymes.

KEY:

} = Prefix deletion. If you delete letters to the left of this, a new or variant quasinayme is revealed on the right.
{ = Postfix deletion. If you delete letters to the right of this, a new or variant quasinayme is revealed on the left.
{} = Midsection deletion. Letters surrounded by curly braces, if deleted, will reveal a new or variant quasinayme.
] = Prefix prepend. Letters typed before a close bracket symbol were not in the generated quasinayme output; but if added, they will form a new or alternate quasinayme.
[ = Prefix append. Letters typed after an open bracket are in the same situation as "Prefix prepend--" but they are appended, not prepended.
[] = Midsection insertion. Letters surrounded by brackets indicate a possible insertion which will form a variant or new quasinayme.
/a/i/ = Variation. Letters partitioned by slashes show possible alternate letters, which form variant quasinaymes. For example, GleeL/o/i/a denotes two variants, one with an "o," the other with an "i."
() = Invention. Names in parentheses are original creations of mine (usually inspired by a discarded quasinayme, or substantial modification of a generated quasinayme).
? = I wonder if this name has in fact not been bestowed on anyone in real life. If we can be quite sure it has not, it deserves top marks for horrible plausibility. We might also expect it to eventually proliferate in real life.
|comment| = Comments.

Notes:

If the end of a name has a closing curly brace } (or in other words, the whole original name may be treated as a long prefix, which may be deleted), and this is followed by an invented name (in parenthesis), this indicates that while I discarded the original name, it inspired another, original name.

Where it is impractical to list all of the detected variants formed by various deletions, all these variants are listed after the original name, {enclosed in curly braces}.

If a retained name is followed by an invented one, it means the invented one was inspired by the retained one.

An underscore next to a deletion marker, for example _} or {_ means that I don't consider the originally generated name plausible unless the portion marked for deletion *is* deleted. Still, I like to note what the computer thought; otherwise I'd not keep any record of the deletion.

An underscore next to an insertion marker works the same as for a deletion marker (or, I consider the quasinayme implausible without the insertion).

To add misery to variations, any variant which was original to a generated word is noted by an underscore prefix; for example: GleeLoa was the originally generated quasinayme, and because the "i" alternate for "o" occured to me, I note that the "o" was the original letter, by prefixing it with an underscore, like this: GleeL/_o/i/a.

More complex mark-up examples:

DeEll}{annysa; the original name is DeEllannysa, but if you delete everything before OR after the curly braces, you get a new name: DeEll OR Annysa.

Patrae}{L}izon; the original name is PatraeLizon (which makes me shudder), but by deleting various lengths of the beginning or end, you get three new names: Patrae, Lizon, and Patrael.

Most names can be read with an imaginary appended [a, [ah, [la, [el, or [lu, [elle, or [ette, or those can be inserted near anywhere in the name, for extraneous feminine embellishment.

For names purposes, you may usually imagine a wide variety of consonants or vowels partitioned with a wide variety of variant letters; for example /d/x/ or /m/n/l/, or /a/y/, etc., ad naseum . . .

Regarding female names, you can take nearly any real name, or "special" word, and append a feminine suffix, e.g.

Any-choice-word/[a]/[lyn/lin[da]]/[l]e[tt/ll][a/e], and in some cases [er].

Deletions, insertions, variations, and inventions may all be freely intermingled. For example, /a/i/ alternates may be intermingled with brackets, as in R]/a/i/[La, or with curly braces, etc. ad naseum.

Some generated names recombobulated from femenine sources appear masculine, and visa-versa. These are indicated with arrows and either "fem." or "masc," with other-gender alternatives listed beside them in parentheses.

These aren't alphabetized yet.

Quasinaymes which I rate very highly (for plausible awfulness) are marked with plus marks (+) after them, however many I feel the quasinayme deserves.
